Math Formula Sheet

Memorize structure + when to apply — not just symbols. Where units matter, carry them. When a formula has *conditions* (angle measure units, factoring requirements, domain limits) write those beside it in your scratch work.

Circle Sector & Arc

Sector Area (θ in radians): A = (1/2) r² θ
Sector Area (θ in degrees): A = (θ / 360) π r²
Arc Length (θ in radians): s = r θ
If θ is in degrees → convert: θrad = θdeg · π / 180

Percent Change

% change = ((final − initial) / initial) × 100%

Special Right Triangles

30°–60°–90° sides: x : x√3 : 2x (short : long : hyp)
45°–45°–90° sides: x : x : x√2

Key Geometry

  • Triangle Inequality: |a − b| < c < a + b
  • Area Circle: πr²  •  Circumference: 2πr
  • Central vs Inscribed Angle: central = 2 × (inscribed subtending same arc)
  • Complementary: A + B = 90° ⇒ sin A = cos B, cos A = sin B

Algebra & Quadratics

Linear Forms

  • Standard: Ax + By = C ⇒ slope m = −A/B (B ≠ 0); y‑intercept = C/B
  • No solution (ax + b = cx + d): coefficients of x equal & constants different
  • Infinite solutions: both sides algebraically identical

Quadratic Basics (ax² + bx + c)

Sum of roots: −b / a
Product of roots: c / a
Axis of symmetry: x = −b / (2a)
Vertex x: h = −b / (2a); y = f(h) = (4ac − b²)/(4a)
Discriminant Δ = b² − 4ac:
• Δ < 0 → no real roots
• Δ = 0 → 1 real double root
• Δ > 0 → 2 distinct real roots

Quadratic Formula

x = [ −b ± √(b² − 4ac) ] / (2a)

Factor & Zero Theorems

  • Factor Theorem: (x − a) factor ⇔ f(a) = 0.
  • Use possible rational roots: ±(factors of constant)/(factors of leading coefficient)

Exponential & Functions

Exponential: y = a·b^x, a = initial (y‑intercept), b = growth (b > 1) or decay (0 < b < 1)

Data Distribution Shape (Mean vs Median)

  • Left‑skewed: mean < median
  • Symmetric: mean ≈ median
  • Right‑skewed: mean > median

Math Strategy & Pitfalls

Check Units & Angle Mode: Degrees vs radians in trig / sector formulas. Convert early to avoid recalc.
Extraneous Roots: Always plug back after squaring, rational equations, or absolute value splits.
  • Domain Restrictions: Denominator ≠ 0; radicand of even root ≥ 0; log arguments > 0.
  • Inequalities: Flip sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative.
  • Group Like Terms First: Reduces careless sign errors before solving.
  • Mark Hard vs Medium: Weighted scoring: protect accuracy on medium items before gambles on hard.
  • READ the Question Stem Last Line Twice (units? form? number of solutions?).
Keep a compact personal error log: (Concept • Error Type • Fix) → review before each new full practice.

English / Writing Quick Guide

Goal: identify error category (boundary, agreement, punctuation choice, modifier, parallelism, diction) in under 5 seconds. Then apply minimal rule needed. When in doubt: prefer clarity + concision without changing intended meaning.

Sentence Boundaries

StructureCorrect JoiningNotes
Independent + Independent Period • Semicolon • Comma + FANBOYS • Colon/Dash (if 2nd explains) Colon/dash needs full sentence before it.
Dependent + Independent Dependent , Independent If dependent first → comma; if second → usually no comma.
Comma Splice Fix: replace comma with period, semicolon, or add FANBOYS Comma + pronoun (“..., it...”) = red flag.

Punctuation Essentials

Semicolon

Same strength as a period. Use only between two independent clauses (optionally with a conjunctive adverb: “; however,”).

Colon / Dash (single)

  • Full sentence before; list / explanation / restatement after.
  • Colon vs dash: grammatically interchangeable; dash = stronger emphasis.

Non‑Essential Info

Surround with two: commas, dashes, or parentheses (matching pair). Do not mix.

Core, nonessential, core. ✔
Core—nonessential—core. ✔
Core (nonessential) core. ✔

Lists & Modifiers

  • Series: item, item, and item (Oxford comma optional).
  • Coordinate adjectives (reversible, can insert “and”) need a comma.

Common Comma Misuses

  • No comma between subject and verb.
  • No comma before/after “that.”
  • No comma before a preposition or between compound items.

Clauses & Transitions

Dependent Starters

Although / Because / When / If / Since / While — if they start the sentence, use comma after their clause.

Transitions (Meaning First)

  • Continuers: moreover, for example, in addition
  • Contrast: however, nevertheless, on the other hand
  • Cause/Effect: therefore, consequently, thus

Test tactic: blank out the transition → decide relation (same / opposite / cause) → pick one that matches.

Agreement & Pronouns

Subject–Verb

  • Ignore prepositional phrases & non‑essential clauses between subject and verb.
  • Compound subject (A and B) = plural.
  • Each / Every / Each of = singular.

Pronouns

  • Match number + clarity: replace pronoun with its noun to test.
  • Its = possessive; it’s = it is; their = possessive plural; they’re = they are.

Possessives vs Plurals

Singular possessive: student’s
Plural: students
Plural possessive: students’
Irregular plural possessive: children’s

Verbs & Tense

  • Maintain tense consistency within paragraph context.
  • Present perfect (has/have + past participle): action started past, continues now (“have studied for years”).
  • Past perfect (had + part.): earlier of two past actions (“had studied before she enrolled”). Trigger: “by the time.”
  • Passive often wordier—prefer active unless only passive is grammatical.
  • Infinitive vs -ing: rely on idiomatic usage (“attempt to improve,” not “attempt improving”).

Parallelism, Modifiers, Comparisons

Parallel Lists

All items same grammatical form: noun / noun / noun OR to + verb / to + verb.

Correlative Pairs

either…or | neither…nor | not only…but also | both…and | as…as

Dangling & Misplaced Modifiers

  • Intro phrase must modify the subject that immediately follows.
  • Reposition modifier close to the word it describes.

Comparisons

  • Compare like to like: “the population of City A vs that of City B.”
  • than (comparison) vs then (time). Always check “longer than.”

Miscellaneous & Style

  • Concision: If two choices are grammatical & equal in meaning, shorter usually correct.
  • Idioms / Prepositions: rely on ear: “interested in,” “capable of,” “focus on.”
  • Adjective vs Adverb: modify verb with -ly; “ran quick” ✗ → “ran quickly.”
  • Redundancy: Remove repeated meaning (“unexpected surprise” → “surprise”).
  • Pronoun Case: Try sentence with only the pronoun (“Me went” ✗ → “I went”).
